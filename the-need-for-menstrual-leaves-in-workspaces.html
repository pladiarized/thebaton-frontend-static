The need for menstrual leaves in Workspaces/h2>
Spraha Srivastava

Menstrual leaves or period leaves is a powerful concept that is emerging in Indian workspaces. These leaves call for workspaces that aim to tackle the embedded patriarchy to make them more inclusive. Menstruation, also known as periods, is highly stigmatized in Indian society and is considered taboo. Rather than making women aware and comfortable with their bodies, this biological process is interdicted. Period leaves is an important concept that requires more consideration because, during their menstrual cycle, most women are subjected to intense pain and various other medical conditions such as Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS), dysmenorrhea, heavy blood flow, and nausea, making it a challenging task to work for hours at strain. There is a need for laws to be developed to make the workspaces more inclusive for women and create an accepting and comfortable environment.

Additionally, this would destigmatize the concept of menstruation as it would engage conversation and normalize it in our societal framework. Implementation of period leaves would ensure that workspaces are designed to be more gender-inclusive and sensitized to women's issues. This would further increase women's participation in the workforce. Such laws should be developed and implemented equally throughout the country to ensure that women in rural settings can benefit. There is an urgent need for menstruation to be discussed in mainstream settings due to its suppression from dialogue for years. 

The workspaces in India built on a patriarchal framework are attributed to women joining the workforce late, making them less inclusive of their needs. The essential needs of women, such as the construction of separate toilets, saw the light of the day during the late 1960s with the increase in the participation of women in the working sector. Therefore, there is an urgent need to implement menstrual leaves in such settings to make them more accommodating to the needs of women and allow for an inclusive and comfortable environment. The idea of the implementation of period leaves in India is not a novel concept. The Bihar government has been offering two-day paid period leaves to women employees since 1992. In 2017, Ninong Ering, a Congress MP from Arunachal Pradesh, moved a private person's bill called 'The Menstruation Benefits Bill, 2017' in the parliament to implement a two-day menstrual leave for women. Countries such as Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Indonesia, and Zambia have an existing system of menstrual leaves for their female employees. In India, Zomato recently introduced a ten days per year policy of period leaves in 2020. Other Indian companies such as Culture Machine, Magzter India, and Gozoop also allow their female employees the benefit of period leaves.

Implementing such menstrual leaves in India is a perfectly constitutional subject and does not invalidate the right to equality. Article 15(1) of the Indian Constitution prohibits the state from discriminating against any citizen based on caste, religion, race, sex, or place of birth. However, Article 15(3) provides a provision that allows the state to make special provisions to protect women and children. This is an exception to Article 15(1) and is essential to ensure special provisions by the government to protect certain rights. Additionally, Article 42 of the DPSPs ensures that the state shall make provisions to ensure just and humane working conditions and maternity relief. The idea of granting women period leaves falls well within this constitutional ambit as they must be provided with relief while being subjected to excruciating pain during menstruation. In the Supreme Court case of Andhra Pradesh v PB Vijayakumar, it was held that the clause "special provisions for women" under Article 15(3) allows the state to make provisions to improve the participation of women in the workforce, that can be done either in the form of reservation or affirmative action. Therefore, period leaves in India can be considered a legal and constitutionally valid law to be implemented by the government to ensure women's rights and freedom to work in a friendly and inclusive workspace. 

The notion of period leaves has received equal questioning from critics and many women as they believe it might lead to alienation of women by creating a gender bias. Moreover, it has been argued that it might prove counterproductive to the struggle for achieving equality. It has also been believed that it might lead to companies not hiring women and not being seen at par with cis-gendered men due to the extra leaves. However, what needs to be reconsidered is the patriarchal mindset structure of the workspaces that cater primarily to the needs of men. Instead of debating whether these period leaves are necessary, the focus needs to be diverted to building workspaces that are inclusive and accommodating to the needs of all genders. This idea of equality and the provisions being the same for everyone cannot be applied to a biological process to which cis men are indifferent. Menstruating women cannot be denied their rights despite being aware of menstruation's physical and mental burden. However, these biological differences should not become a source of treating women employees differently, for which workspaces should structure their policies so that they acknowledge and accommodate these biological processes. At the same time, these spaces should allow for different modes of work productivity to ensure there is no discrimination subjected to women. The leaves will ultimately create an equalizing force as they would ensure equality in the working conditions yet at the same time integrate the unique needs of women.

The policy for menstrual leaves needs to be applied to the various structure of labour laws existing in public and private sectors in India. This policy is necessary because it is essential to be highlighted that menstruation is an inevitable biological process that women experience monthly. It is not a choice that is made, and therefore maximum efforts should be made to ensure the protection of the menstrual rights of women. The policy needs to ensure that workspaces adapt to them differently from the capitalist setting they operate in presently. Instead of the quantitative analysis of working hours, labour productivity needs to be given more emphasis. A balancing act needs to be implemented that should also include the provision to work from home. This policy has been tested and proved effective during the COVID 19 pandemic, where most organizations adopted the work from home policy and utilized it to its maximum potential. There is also a need to include workspaces for menstruating women, such as sanitary pad dispensers and hygienic toiletry facilities. Another essential factor that should be considered while formulating a policy on period leaves is that they are implemented uniformly throughout the country to ensure that the same benefits are not solely availed in the organized sector. Menstrual leaves is a proper need to be secured by women in the informal sector, especially those working as labourers. Such policies need to be expanded to the rural areas of the country for them to be effective. Therefore, the government needs to take immediate measures for the implementation of such leaves. In the recent case of Delhi Labour Union through its General Secretary v Union of India, the court directed the authorities, including the Centre and Delhi government, to decide on the PIL filed by the Delhi labour union seeking four days of paid period leave each month. The PIL also sought 45 minutes of rest during the first four days of the menstrual cycle and payment as overtime to women who choose to work during their menstrual cycle. 

Menstrual leaves is a basic human right that should be granted to women and the plethora of other menstrual rights that need protection. This will ensure that these necessary rights get representation in the government policies, ultimately leading to their acknowledgement and awareness. Menstruation needs to be normalized in society as this would ensure consideration and sensitization towards the needs of women. Implementation of period leaves should be seen as a positive development in providing women with proportionate support to accommodate workspaces. This biological reality of women finds mere representation in the country's legal sphere, making it essential to give it its due cognizance to remove the historical stigma.
